ADa Lovelace

Software Engineer

Ada Lovelace was a 19th-century mathematician often cited as the world's first computer programmer. [1, 2] As the daughter of the poet Lord Byron, she was steered toward science and logic to avoid his "wild" influence. [2, 3] She is best known for her work with Charles Babbage on his early mechanical computer, the Analytical Engine. [2, 4]

In her 1843 notes, she wrote an algorithm to calculate Bernoulli numbers, marking the first time a machine's logic was documented. [5, 6] More impressively, she envisioned that computers could go beyond math to create music or art if programmed correctly. [7, 8] This concept of "poetical science" essentially predicted the modern digital age. [8, 9] Today, her legacy lives on through the Ada programming language and Ada Lovelace Day, which celebrates women in STEM. [10, 11]

Note G

Note G is the most famous section of Ada Lovelace’s 1843 commentary on Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine. It is historically monumental because it contains the world’s first published computer program. Lovelace used Note G to provide a detailed, step-by-step algorithm to calculate Bernoulli numbers. She demonstrated how the machine could use loops and subroutines, concepts that remain fundamental to coding today. Though the Engine was never built, her logic has since been proven technically sound. Beyond the math, Note G outlines a revolutionary vision for what computers could become. Lovelace realized that if a machine could manipulate symbols, it could process more than just numbers. She famously suggested that such a machine might one day compose elaborate music or create scientific art. However, Note G also contains "Lady Lovelace’s Objection." She argued that the machine had "no pretensions to originate anything," meaning it could only do what humans programmed it to do. This sparked a debate about Artificial Intelligence that continues to this day. In short, Note G transformed the Analytical Engine from a calculator into the blueprint for a general-purpose computer.
